Machine Learning Fundamentals

Now that you understand what AI is, let's explore how machines actually learn. We'll cover the three types of machine learning, the ML workflow, and key concepts you'll use throughout your AI journey.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this topic, students will be able to:

  • Explain how machine learning differs from traditional programming
  • Distinguish between supervised, unsupervised, and reinforcement learning
  • Understand the basic ML workflow: data ‚Üí model ‚Üí predictions
  • Recognize key ML terminology and concepts
  • Identify which type of ML to use for different problems

Opening & Bridge from Topic 1

(2 minutes)

"Great! So now we understand what AI is - it's machines performing intelligent tasks. But here's the million-dollar question: HOW do we teach machines to be intelligent?

This is where Machine Learning comes in. And I want to start with a fundamental shift in thinking...

Traditional Programming vs. Machine Learning:

Programming Paradigm Shift

Traditional Programming: Rules + Data ‚Üí Answers Example: You write exact rules for a spam filter - IF email contains "FREE MONEY" ‚Üí Spam - IF email from boss ‚Üí Not Spam Machine Learning: Data + Answers ‚Üí Rules Example: Show ML system 10,000 emails labeled spam/not spam - ML figures out the patterns automatically - Discovers rules you never explicitly programmed

Think about teaching a child vs. programming a robot:

  • Teaching a child to identify animals: Show pictures, they learn patterns
  • Programming traditional software: Write exact rules for every scenario

ML is more like teaching the child!

Part 1: The Three Main Types of Machine Learning

(12 minutes)

Slide: Types of Machine Learning

Three Main Approaches: 1. Supervised Learning ‚Üí Learning from labeled examples 2. Unsupervised Learning ‚Üí Finding patterns without labels 3. Reinforcement Learning ‚Üí Learning through trial and error

1. SUPERVISED LEARNING (5 minutes)

Supervised Learning = Learning with a Teacher

Process: 1. Show the model examples WITH correct answers 2. Model learns the pattern 3. Model predicts answers for new examples Like: Teaching with flashcards ‚Üí Front: Picture of cat ‚Üí Back: "This is a CAT" ‚úì

"Let's start with Supervised Learning - the most common type of ML you'll use.

The Core Concept:
Imagine you're studying for an exam with flashcards. Each card has a question on front and the answer on back. You study many cards, learn the patterns, and then can answer new questions. That's exactly how supervised learning works!

Real Example - Email Spam Detection:

Step 1: Training Data (Learning Phase)

  • Email 1: "Get rich quick!!!" ‚Üí SPAM ‚úì
  • Email 2: "Meeting at 3pm" ‚Üí NOT SPAM ‚úì
  • Email 3: "Click here to win $$$" ‚Üí SPAM ‚úì
  • Email 4: "Your package delivered" ‚Üí NOT SPAM ‚úì
  • ... (thousands more examples)

Step 2: The Model Learns
The ML algorithm studies these examples and learns: - Words like "rich", "win", "click here" often appear in spam - Exclamation marks and dollar signs are spam indicators - Normal work vocabulary suggests not spam

Step 3: Prediction (The Real Work)
New Email: "FREE iPhone! Click now!!!"
Model predicts: 98% chance it's SPAM

Two Main Tasks in Supervised Learning

Classification (Predicting Categories): ‚Ä¢ Is this email spam or not? (2 categories) ‚Ä¢ What digit is this? 0-9 (10 categories) ‚Ä¢ What animal is in this image? (many categories) ‚Ä¢ Will this customer buy our product? (Yes/No) Regression (Predicting Numbers): ‚Ä¢ What will this house sell for? ($350,000) ‚Ä¢ How many products will we sell next month? (1,247 units) ‚Ä¢ What will the temperature be tomorrow? (72¬∞F)

Everyday Examples of Supervised Learning

  • Your Phone's Face ID: Trained on your face photos (labeled as YOU)
  • Netflix Recommendations: Trained on movies you rated (labeled with your ratings)
  • Voice Assistants: Trained on speech recordings (labeled with what was said)
  • Medical Diagnosis: Trained on X-rays (labeled with diseases)

2. UNSUPERVISED LEARNING (4 minutes)

Unsupervised Learning = Finding Hidden Patterns

Process: 1. Give model data WITHOUT labels 2. Model finds structure and patterns 3. Groups similar things together Like: Organizing a messy room ‚Üí You naturally group similar items ‚Üí No one told you the "right" categories

"Now, what if you don't have labels? What if you just have a bunch of data and want to find patterns? That's Unsupervised Learning!

The Core Concept:
Imagine you walk into a room with 1,000 different objects scattered everywhere. Nobody tells you how to organize them, but you naturally start grouping: - All the books together - All the tools together - All the toys together

You found structure without a teacher. That's unsupervised learning!

Real Example - Customer Segmentation:

A company has data on 100,000 customers: - Age, location, purchase history, browsing behavior

Instead of labeling each customer, they use unsupervised learning and the algorithm discovers 5 groups:

  • Group 1: Young urban professionals, buy tech
  • Group 2: Parents, buy children's items
  • Group 3: Retirees, buy healthcare products
  • Group 4: Students, buy budget items
  • Group 5: Luxury shoppers, buy premium brands

Nobody told the algorithm these categories existed - it found them!

Common Unsupervised Tasks

Clustering (Grouping Similar Things): ‚Ä¢ Customer segmentation (as above) ‚Ä¢ Organizing news articles by topic ‚Ä¢ Grouping similar genes in biology ‚Ä¢ Finding communities in social networks Dimensionality Reduction (Simplifying Complex Data): ‚Ä¢ Taking 1,000 measurements and finding the 10 most important ones ‚Ä¢ Compressing images while keeping important features ‚Ä¢ Visualizing high-dimensional data Anomaly Detection (Finding Weird Stuff): ‚Ä¢ Detecting fraud (unusual transactions) ‚Ä¢ Finding defective products in manufacturing ‚Ä¢ Identifying network intrusions in cybersecurity

3. REINFORCEMENT LEARNING (3 minutes)

Reinforcement Learning = Learning Through Trial and Error

Process: 1. Agent takes actions in environment 2. Gets rewards (good) or penalties (bad) 3. Learns which actions lead to rewards Like: Training a dog ‚Üí Good behavior: Treat! ü¶¥ ‚Üí Bad behavior: No treat ‚Üí Dog learns to maximize treats

"The third major type is Reinforcement Learning - and this one is fascinating because it's most similar to how humans and animals learn!

The Core Concept:
Think about how you learned to ride a bike: - Pedal steady ‚Üí Stay upright ‚Üí Feels good! ‚úì - Lean too far ‚Üí Fall down ‚Üí Ouch! ‚úó - Turn handlebars ‚Üí Stay balanced ‚Üí Success! ‚úì

Through trial and error, you learned which actions lead to good outcomes. That's reinforcement learning!

Real Example - Game Playing AI:

Teaching AI to play video games:

  • Initial State: AI character in game, doesn't know anything
  • Trial 1: Move right ‚Üí Falls in pit ‚Üí Penalty: -10 points (Learns: Moving right in this situation = bad)
  • Trial 2: Jump ‚Üí Avoids pit ‚Üí Reward: +5 points (Learns: Jumping here = good)
  • After millions of trials: AI becomes expert gamer!

Key Components

1. Agent: The learner (the AI) 2. Environment: The world it operates in (the game) 3. Actions: Things it can do (move, jump, shoot) 4. Rewards: Feedback on actions (points, wins, losses) 5. Goal: Maximize total rewards over time

Famous Reinforcement Learning Examples

  • AlphaGo (Google DeepMind): Learned to play Go through self-play, beat world champion in 2016
  • Self-Driving Cars: Learn optimal driving through simulation
  • Robotics: Teaching robots to walk, grasp objects, assemble products
  • Game AI: OpenAI's Dota 2 AI, DeepMind's StarCraft AI

"Now that you know the TYPES of ML, let's talk about HOW you actually build an ML system. Every ML project follows this workflow:

Step 1: COLLECT DATA (The Foundation)
'Garbage in, garbage out' - this is THE most important step!

Example: Building a House Price Predictor
Need data on houses: - Size (square feet) - Location (neighborhood) - Bedrooms/bathrooms - Age of house - Recent sale price ‚Üê This is what we want to predict!

Step 2: PREPARE DATA (The Unglamorous Work)
This takes 60-80% of a data scientist's time!

Common Tasks: - Cleaning: Handle missing values, remove duplicates, fix errors - Transformation: Convert categories to numbers, scale features - Splitting: Training set (80%), Test set (20%)

Why Split Data?
Imagine studying for a test: You wouldn't memorize the exact test questions. You study examples, then take a NEW test. Same with ML - train on some data, test on OTHER data.

Steps 3-7 Overview

Step 3: CHOOSE MODEL (The Algorithm) Pick the right tool for the job! ‚Ä¢ House prices (regression): Linear Regression, Random Forest ‚Ä¢ Image classification: Convolutional Neural Network ‚Ä¢ Customer grouping: K-Means Clustering Step 4: TRAIN MODEL (The Learning) Training Loop: 1. Model makes predictions on training data 2. Compare predictions to actual answers 3. Calculate error (how wrong was it?) 4. Adjust model parameters to reduce error 5. Repeat thousands/millions of times Step 5: EVALUATE MODEL (The Test) Test on NEW data the model never saw ‚Ä¢ Actual price: $350,000 ‚Ä¢ Model predicts: $345,000 ‚Ä¢ Error: $5,000 (pretty good!) Step 6: DEPLOY & USE (The Real World) Put your model into production! ‚Ä¢ Web API, Mobile app, Cloud service, Embedded device Step 7: MONITOR & IMPROVE (The Ongoing Work) ML is never "done"! ‚Ä¢ Watch for performance degradation ‚Ä¢ Collect feedback, gather new data ‚Ä¢ Retrain model periodically

Part 3: Key ML Terminology

(5 minutes)

Essential ML Vocabulary

Features: Input variables (house size, location) Labels: Output we want to predict (house price) Model: The algorithm that learns patterns Parameters: Values the model learns during training Training: Process of learning from data Inference/Prediction: Using trained model on new data Overfitting: Model too specific to training data Underfitting: Model too simple to capture patterns Accuracy: How often model is correct Loss: How wrong the model is

"Let's nail down the vocabulary you'll hear constantly in ML:

FEATURES (inputs, predictors, X)
The information you feed into your model

LABELS (outputs, targets, Y)
What you're trying to predict

OVERFITTING vs UNDERFITTING
Think of it like studying:

  • Underfitting: Barely studied - Student fails because understanding is too simple
  • Good Fit: Studied appropriately - Can solve NEW problems on test
  • Overfitting: Memorized exact homework answers - Fails on DIFFERENT questions

In ML: - Underfitting: Too simple, high error on everything - Good Fit: Low error on training AND test data - Overfitting: Low error on training, high error on test

Common Student Questions & Answers

Q: "Which type of ML is most important to learn?"
A: "Supervised learning is used in about 80% of real-world applications, so we'll spend most time there. But all three types are valuable - reinforcement learning is huge in robotics and games, unsupervised learning is critical for exploratory data analysis."
Q: "How do I know which algorithm to use?"
A: "Great question! There are guidelines (which we'll learn), but often it's trial and error. Try a few, see which works best. Experience helps a lot. The good news: modern libraries make it easy to try multiple algorithms quickly."
Q: "Can I build ML models without understanding the math?"
A: "Yes! Just like you can drive a car without being a mechanical engineer. We'll build intuition for how things work, and you can go deeper into math later if you want. Understanding > Memorizing equations."
Q: "How much data do I need?"
A: "It depends! Simple problems: hundreds to thousands. Complex problems (like image recognition): thousands to millions. But don't let lack of data stop you from starting - even small datasets teach you a lot."
Q: "Is more data always better?"
A: "Generally yes, but with diminishing returns. Quality matters more than quantity. Clean, relevant data beats huge amounts of messy data. Also, more data requires more computation."
